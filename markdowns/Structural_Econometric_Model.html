What Are Structural Econometric Models?

Structural econometric models combine explicit economic theories with statistical models

  • Economic theories are used to develop mathematical statements about how a set of observable “endogenous” variables, yyy, are related to another set of observable “explanatory” variables, xxx.
    • Eg., y=g(x,ξ,Θ)y = g(x, \xi, \Theta)y=g(x,ξ,Θ) , or y≥g(x,ξ,Θ)y \geq g(x, \xi, \Theta)y≥g(x,ξ,Θ), where g(⋅)g(\cdot)g(⋅) is a known function and Θ\ThetaΘ is a set of unknown parameters or functions.
  • Statistical models offer statistical assumptions, and estimation equations.
    • Eg., assume the joint distribution of x,ξx, \xix,ξ and other unobservables
    • Eg., maximize the likelihood or moments.

Make a Comparison With Nonstructural Econometric Models

  • Nonstructural econometric models may not use formal statistical models.
    • Eg., measurement studies that focus on constructing and summarizing data.
      • Labor force participation and unemployment rates.
  • Nonstructural econometric models may use formal statistical models.
    • E.g., autoregressive conditional volatility models.
      • It was used to document an inverse relationship between United Kingdom unemployment rates and changes in wage rates.
  • Nonstructural econometric models ultimately estimate characteristics of the joint population density of xxx and yyy, i.e., f(x,y)f(x, y)f(x,y).
    • E.g., the best linear predictor (BLP(y∣x)BLP(y | x)BLP(y∣x)) of yyy given xxx.
